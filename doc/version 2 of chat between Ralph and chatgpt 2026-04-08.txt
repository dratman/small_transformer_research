What seems to be emerging is a sense that we are trying to understand a system that sits at the intersection of several kinds of structure, none of which are fully understood on their own, and which do not line up cleanly with one another.
There is, first, the original intuition drawn from compilers. In that setting, everything is anchored by a shared formal object. A grammar—say in BNF—describes both the language and the mechanism that processes it. The recursive descent parser is not just compatible with the grammar; it is almost forced by it. That alignment gives a kind of transparency. You know what the system is doing because the description of the language and the description of the computation are, in a deep sense, the same thing.
Nothing like that currently exists for transformers.
The transformer is a highly structured machine, but its structure is not symbolic in the usual way. It operates almost entirely in high-dimensional continuous spaces. The only discrete elements appear at the boundaries—tokenization at the input, and sampling or selection at the output. Everything in between is shaped by the requirements of backpropagation: differentiability, continuity, and smooth dependence on parameters. This is a very strong restriction. It forces us into a regime where traditional computer science constructs—branching, discrete state transitions, explicit control flow—do not naturally appear.
At the same time, natural language itself is not a clean formal object. It is not like a programming language, where the grammar can be specified and enforced. It emerges from biological systems—speech production, hearing, cognition—that are themselves messy and only partially understood. Language is shaped by the vocal tract, by auditory perception, by historical accident, by social use. It is full of redundancy, ambiguity, and irregularity. It is not designed to be convenient for analysis. It is not, as you put it, polite.
Moreover, the medium we train on—text—is already a projection of something richer. Spoken language is fundamentally acoustic: a continuous signal unfolding in time, shaped by coarticulation and perception. Text is a discretization of that signal, a kind of compressed residue. Some of the structure we observe in text may reflect constraints that originate in that underlying physical process, not in an abstract symbolic system.
So we are dealing with a layered situation:
a biological system producing language
a discretized representation of that system (text)
a high-dimensional continuous model trained on that representation
And we are trying to relate all three.
This is where the difficulty becomes more acute. The transformer maps a low-dimensional, combinatorial object—token sequences—into a very high-dimensional continuous space, and then back again. Whatever structure the model uses lives primarily in that internal space. But that space does not come with obvious symbolic landmarks. A “word,” a “subject,” or a “reference” may not correspond to any localized or even stable entity. They may be distributed patterns, shifting from layer to layer.
At the same time, if we want to ask questions about meaning or reference, we need some kind of linguistic framework. Without it, the questions themselves become vague. To ask “what is the semantic reference of this sentence?” presupposes that such a thing is well-defined. Without at least a working notion of reference, we risk using language that sounds precise but is not anchored.
So there is a double gap. We lack a fully satisfactory theory of natural language, and we lack a clear account of how transformer representations relate to anything like such a theory. Bridging both gaps at once is a large task.
Your analogy to early radio engineering captures the situation well, especially in its historical sense. It is not that transformers correspond directly to superheterodyne receivers, but that we may be in a similar phase: systems that work, components that are partly understood, but no unifying framework that makes the whole thing transparent. And even in a mature field like radio engineering, full transparency is not really achieved. There is always a mixture of theory, empirical tuning, and practical knowledge.
In the case of transformers, one might go further and think of the system as a kind of dynamical process in a very high-dimensional space. The state of the model evolves layer by layer, influenced by attention (which mixes information across positions) and nonlinearities (which bend the space). There is no explicit control flow—no program counter, no branching. Instead, there is something like a continuous, state-dependent routing of information. If one insists on an analogy, it begins to look more like a flow than a program: a trajectory through a learned vector field in thousands of dimensions.
That picture is suggestive, but it does not yet give us a usable theory. In ordinary fluid dynamics, the equations are known and the space is low-dimensional. Here, the “flow” is learned, the space is enormous, and we have no governing equations in a form we can analyze. It is, as you said, “a simple matter of hydrodynamics in a few thousand dimensions”—but without the usual tools of hydrodynamics.
The success of backpropagation reinforces this situation. Because we rely on gradient-based optimization, we are effectively committed to continuous, differentiable models. That excludes, or at least complicates, the use of discrete symbolic structures internally. The addition of nonlinearities makes matters worse from an analytical point of view. If the system were linear, we could decompose and understand it more easily. But with nonlinear layers stacked deeply, the system becomes piecewise and input-dependent in a way that resists clean global analysis. Our familiar mathematical tools begin to falter.
What seems to be missing is something analogous to what Galois provided in another context: a shift to the right level of abstraction. Not more detailed descriptions of the existing machinery, but a way of organizing it so that large classes of behavior become intelligible at once. That would likely involve identifying invariants—structures that are preserved across transformations—and understanding how the model acts on them. But it is not clear what those invariants should be, or how they would connect to language as we understand it.
Given all this, it seems unlikely that a single, clean unifying theory will appear quickly, if at all. A more realistic expectation is a gradual accumulation of partial understandings: stable patterns, recurring mechanisms, useful abstractions that explain some aspects of behavior under controlled conditions. Your approach—small models, controlled data, restartable experiments—fits well with that. It allows one to isolate phenomena and test hypotheses in a setting where variables can be managed.
The broader picture, then, is not one of imminent clarity but of a long, uneven path. There may well be significant conceptual advances along the way, but they are difficult to predict. If a unifying framework does emerge, it may look quite different from the symbolic grammars or formal systems we are accustomed to. It may combine elements of geometry, information flow, and linguistic structure in a way that is not yet visible.
So the expectation of a long road ahead seems justified. That is not a counsel of pessimism, but a recognition of the complexity of the object we are trying to understand.
